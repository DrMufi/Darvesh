ULO 1: Receptive Language Development

Listening Skills

Listening is the foundation of communication. Children understand language before they can speak, making listening development crucial for social interactions, learning, and thinking.

Understanding Listening Development

Listening skills, also called receptive language, involve understanding spoken words, following directions, and processing information. Children develop listening skills from birth through attentive, responsive interactions.

Children understand many more words than they can say

Key Theories Explained Simply

Skinner's Behaviorist Theory: Children learn through repetition, imitation, and positive reinforcement. When you consistently model good listening and respond positively to your child's communication, they learn these patterns (Skinner, 1957).

Chomsky's Nativist Theory: Children are born with an innate ability to learn language—a "Language Acquisition Device." Your role is to provide a rich language environment that activates this natural capacity (Chomsky, 1965).

How You Support Listening

  • Get down to their level when speaking
  • Use clear, simple language appropriate for their age
  • Give processing time (wait 5-10 seconds after asking)
  • Read aloud daily with expression and engagement
  • Play listening games like "Simon Says" or sound hunts

Real-Life Example: Story Time

The Situation

You're reading "The Very Hungry Caterpillar" with your 3-year-old.

Your Supportive Approach

Pause at pictures, ask "What do you think happens next?", use different voices for characters, let your child turn pages, and connect the story to their experiences ("Remember when we ate strawberries?").

What This Achieves

  • Develops listening comprehension
  • Builds vocabulary through context
  • Encourages prediction and thinking skills
  • Makes reading a joyful, shared experience
