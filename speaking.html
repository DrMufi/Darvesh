ULO 1: Language Development Theories

Speaking Skills

Understanding how children develop speaking abilities helps us support their journey from first sounds to confident conversations. This section translates key theories into practical knowledge for families.

What is Speaking Development?

Speaking development is the gradual process through which children learn to express their thoughts, needs, and feelings using words and sentences. It begins with early cooing and babbling and evolves into complex language use.

Children learn language through meaningful interactions with caring adults

Key Theories Explained Simply

Vygotsky's Social Learning Theory: Children learn language best through social interaction with more knowledgeable others (like parents and caregivers). Your guidance helps your child reach their next level of understanding—what Vygotsky called the "Zone of Proximal Development."

Bruner's Language Acquisition Support System (LASS): Adults naturally create supportive "scaffolds" during conversations that help children learn. When you simplify your speech, repeat words, and expand on what your child says, you're providing essential support (Bruner, 1983).

How You Support Speaking

  • Respond warmly to your child's attempts to communicate
  • Expand their words ("Ball!" → "Yes, that's a big red ball!")
  • Model clear speech at a natural pace
  • Create conversation opportunities during daily routines
  • Celebrate effort over perfection

Real-Life Example: Mealtime Conversations

The Situation

Your 2-year-old points at an apple and says "App!"

Your Supportive Response

"Yes! That's an apple. It's a red apple. Would you like to eat the apple? Let's wash the apple first. The apple feels smooth and cold."

What This Achieves

  • Validates their communication attempt
  • Expands vocabulary (apple, red, smooth, cold)
  • Models full sentences
  • Creates a meaningful conversation
