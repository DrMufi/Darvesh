ULO 1: Developmental Progressions

Developmental Progression

Every child develops at their own pace, but understanding typical milestones helps you celebrate progress and know when to seek support. These progressions show how speaking and listening skills develop over time.

0–12 Months

The Foundation Stage

Speaking

  • Cooing and babbling (6-9 months)
  • First words like "mama" or "dada" (9-12 months)
  • Uses gestures (pointing, waving)
  • Responds to their name

Listening

  • Startles at loud sounds
  • Turns toward familiar voices
  • Recognizes common words (bottle, no)
  • Enjoys songs and rhymes

Your Role: Talk to your baby during everyday activities. Narrate what you're doing, respond to their sounds, and create a warm, language-rich environment. This builds the foundation for all future learning.

1–3 Years

The Explosion Stage

Speaking

  • Vocabulary grows rapidly (50-200 words by age 2)
  • Combines two words ("more milk")
  • Asks simple questions ("what's that?")
  • Uses language to express needs and feelings

Listening

  • Follows simple instructions
  • Points to named pictures in books
  • Understands simple questions
  • Enjoys listening to stories

Your Role: Expand on their words, read together daily, sing songs, and give them time to express themselves. Avoid correcting grammar—model the correct form instead. This supports their social interactions and thinking skills.

3–5 Years

The Refinement Stage

Speaking

  • Uses complex sentences
  • Tells stories and recounts events
  • Asks "why" and "how" questions
  • Speech is mostly clear to strangers

Listening

  • Follows multi-step instructions
  • Understands concepts like size, color, shape
  • Listens to longer stories
  • Can retell familiar stories

Your Role: Engage in extended conversations, ask open-ended questions, introduce new vocabulary through books and experiences, and support their curiosity about language. This prepares them for school success.

Remember: Every Child is Unique

These milestones are general guidelines based on research. Children develop at different rates, and that's perfectly normal. If you have concerns about your child's development, speak with your healthcare provider or early childhood professional. Australian early childhood services can provide additional support.
